# my-first-repo

A practice sandbox for the **AWS MLU Faculty Fellowship** at Delaware State University, College of Agriculture, Science & Technology.

> This repository exists so you can fork it, edit it, and make your first commit — all in the browser. It is intentionally small and friendly.

## Welcome, faculty.

You are looking at the repository you will fork in **Workshop One: Git in the Browser**. After you click the **Fork** button, this becomes *your* version. Anything you change in your fork stays in your fork. The original is untouched.

## What to do here

1. **Fork** this repository (top-right of this page).
2. **Edit** the README in your fork — add a line at the top, something that reminds you of what you will learn next session.
3. **Commit** that change with a short, descriptive message.
4. **Find your commit** in the **Commits** tab. That's your fingerprint on the history.

## What comes after this

In Workshop Two, you will encounter [PartyRock](https://partyrock.aws) — AWS's no-code generative AI playground. The SPICE prompts you write there are exactly the kind of artifact faculty collect and refine over time, and your fork of this repo is a natural home for that collection.

A [`NOTES.md`](./NOTES.md) file lives in this repo as a starter. Use it however helps you most.

## What you will do in later workshops

In **Workshop Two and beyond**, you will move into AWS SageMaker Studio. There you will run:

```
git clone https://github.com/aws-samples/aws-mlu-eep-generative-ai.git
```

That command is the same idea as the fork you will perform today, just applied to the actual course repository inside a terminal. You don't need to do it now. We will do that together when you are in Workshop Studio.

## Three rules of public-repo hygiene

1. **Every commit is permanent.** Even after you delete a file, the commit history still contains it. Treat every commit as public the moment you click the button.
2. **No identifiable student data, ever.** No names, no grades, no IEP/504 information, no accommodation records, nothing FERPA-protected.
3. **No institutional secrets.** No internal budgets, personnel files, private policy drafts, or unpublished research data.
